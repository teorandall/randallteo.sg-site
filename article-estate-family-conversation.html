The Family Conversation Nobody Wants to Have — and Why You Should Have It Anyway

July 2026
5 min read
Back to Estate & Legacy

By the team at randallteo.sg

Most families in Singapore do not talk about money, death, or inheritance. It feels uncomfortable, presumptuous, or simply too morbid to bring up at the dinner table.

But the families who do have these conversations, even imperfectly and even awkwardly, are far better prepared when something actually happens. And something always eventually happens.

Why people avoid it

The reasons are understandable. Nobody wants to seem like they are waiting for someone to die. Parents worry that talking about inheritance will create conflict between their children. Adult children worry that bringing it up will upset their parents or seem greedy.

So nothing gets said. Important documents go unmade. And when a parent passes or loses capacity, the family is left making decisions under pressure, with incomplete information, and sometimes with very different assumptions about what their loved one would have wanted.

What actually happens when there is no conversation

Without clear instructions, families are left guessing. Who should handle the finances? What did mum want to happen to the flat? Did dad want to be kept on life support? Should the business be sold or passed on?

These are not abstract questions. They are decisions that fall on real people, under real stress, often while grieving. When there is no clarity, even close families can fracture over disagreements that could have been prevented by a single honest conversation.

What a good conversation actually covers

It does not need to be formal or comprehensive all at once. A starting point might simply be:

01
Where are the important documents kept? Does anyone know where to find the Will, the LPA, the insurance policies?
02
Who should be called first if something happens? Is there a financial adviser, a lawyer, a doctor who should be contacted?
03
What are your wishes around medical care? Do you want to be kept alive at all costs, or are there limits to what you would want?
04
Is there anything you want to pass on that is not financial? Specific items, letters, instructions for how you want to be remembered.

How to start

You do not need to have all the answers. You just need to open the door. For some families, a practical trigger helps. Reviewing an insurance policy together, updating a Will, or setting up an LPA can naturally lead into a broader conversation.

For others, a third party helps. Having a financial planner or estate planning professional present can take the emotional charge out of the conversation and keep things focused and productive. Sometimes just starting with yourself is enough to get the ball rolling.
